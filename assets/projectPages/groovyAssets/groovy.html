Groovy Groceries

UX Design & Research | Aug-Oct 2021, Apr-Aug 2022 (revisit and finalize)

Groovy Groceries is a university grocery app that helps college students find ways to use their school dining dollars, budget their spendings, plan out meals, and grocery shop with ease.

My Role: UX Designer

User research, affinity diagramming, empathy map, user persona, user flow, info architecture, low-high fidelity wireframing, and prototyping

Tools:

Figma and Miro

Team:

Emily Song (UX Designer)

Design Question

How might we help student stay on top of their grocery needs?

Research

I conduct user surveys to answer the following key areas of interest:

  1. What are notable preferences and barriers that can impact a student's grocery, meal planning, and budgeting experience?
  2. Understand users' shopping, meal planning, and budgeting processes.

I included both qualitative (free-response) and quantitative (multiple choice) questions within our survey to see how each user differs in their approach to buying groceries, budgeting, and meal planning.

View Survey Responses

Intended respondents: University Students
Survey respondents: Individuals from diverse background and age-groups

Method Limitations:

  • 11 respondents is not a large sample size.
  • Due to sending out the survey to online forums with diverse users, we got a lot of variation in responses and not a lot of responses specifically from university students.

Even though there are limitations with the data we gathered, there are still valuable responses we wanted to consider. So, in order to observe patterns more succinctly, I created an Affinity Diagram to organize and consolidate commonalities and differences between responses.

6 Main Findings

Using the Affinity Diagram, I grouped common patterns into a few main findings:

82% of individuals cook at home, at least once a week
Shoppers make a physical or mental list of what they need before going out
Meal planning is often spontaneously done, depending on busyness and the groceries available at home
Shoppers stated grocery store proximity, quality of produce, and financially within budget as their top shopping priorities
Top frustrations with grocery shopping include missing products, checkout lines, and physical setbacks such as inability to carry certain produce
Many shoppers do not have a set method for budgeting on a regular basis

Empathy Map

From the survey findings, I created an empathy map to categorize the data into a more comprehensive diagram that can be used to represent our users' needs, wants, actions, and thought processes.

User Persona

Informed by the empathy map, I then made a user persona to gain a better understanding of our product's user, who they are, what they want and need, and painpoints they encounter.

Information Architecture

I then created an information architecture of our product's layout in order to map out how users will be navigating from one experience flow to another.

Low-fidelity Wireframes

Using the information architecture, I created low-fidelity wireframes to translate our user experience flows into our product's beginning visual concepts, and to further develop our early design decisions.

First Iteration of High Fidelity Wireframes

Initial Iteration High-fidelity Wireframes

From the low-fidelity wireframes, I created the first iteration of high-fidelity wireframes for the product's main screens.

Revision of High-fidelity Wireframes

From the above high-fidelity wireframes, I decided to iterate on three main flows: shopping, meal planning, and budgeting.

In my initial high-fidelity wireframes, many of my colour and font usage were not organized due to lacking a succinct design hierarchy. For the new iteration of the shopping, meal planning, and budgeting flows, I simplified down our palette to two colours. I reconsidered our fonts, and created a typography ramp with varying text hierarchy.

Additionally, I reiterated the user-flow and aspects of UI elements to further improve the experience and the product's visual cohesion.

Grocery Shopping

For the new shopping flow, the accent green colour is now placed specifically on call-to-action elements. Now users can more efficiently and distinctly tell where those elements are simply from a quick glance at the screen.

For the grocery item page ("Apple" screen) and the My Cart page, I brought more attention to the primary buttons and elements on the screen through making each of the screen background colour be the primary grey as opposed to the harsh white used in the past. I added tabs for listing ingredients and nutritional information when viewing a specific grocery item. Additionally, for the My Cart screen I enlarged the Pickup and Delivery buttons for greater visibility.

Meal Planning

For the new Meal Planning flow, each day of the week is now accessible through selection buttons at the top of the screen, as opposed to students needing to scroll to access each day in the previous design iteration. Now students can quickly navigate between days and weeks from the top of the screen.

I made the background our primary grey to visually bring out the meal card elements more. I placed our main "add a plan" button more prominently at the bottom right of the screen for students to access that easily. Since our weekly & daily plans can be accessed at the top of the screen, the calendar button that used to be next to the "add a plan" button can be removed completely.

Budgeting

For this new iteration of the budgeting flow, I redesigned the weekly spending display to be a visual circle as opposed to the previous design's pie-chart so that users can now quickly understand their weekly spendings with a simple glance.

I redesigned the Weekly Activities bar graph to show the exact amount of dollars spent per category, as opposed to the older design's percentage measurement. Dollar amount brings more clarity and a concrete understanding to a user's money spending.

I added a new "Detailed Transactions" screen to the budgeting flow to allow users a greater understanding of their monthly spending, and to give a clear list of their past transactions. This was not in the prior design iteration, but is quite a necessary addition to a student's budgeting experience.

What I Learned

Grocery purchases, planning, and budgeting can vary diversely between our users. I aimed to create a solution to allow our users the flexibility and efficiency of addressing their particular need, whether that be all experiences this product has to offer or portions of it. Through iterating on specific flows of our solution multiple times, this greatly allowed me to further think through our users' experiences within that particular aspect of our overall solution.

Future Improvements

In the future I would want to achieve a majority of our respondents to be university students within our research, and to incorporate more user interviews within a design cycle. Through doing so, we could gather more feedback on our design and iterate further on those reviews.
